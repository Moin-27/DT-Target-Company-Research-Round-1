# Plan for Building a 1000-Company Target List

## Why This Needs a Plan at All

Building 1,000 companies that actually fit an ICP is harder than it sounds. Anyone can pull 1,000 company names from IndiaMART in a day. The problem is that most of them won't qualify — they'll be traders, distributors, companies with no real manufacturing, or businesses in completely irrelevant sectors. So the question isn't really "how do I find 1,000 companies" — it's "how do I find 1,000 companies that are actually worth something."

The answer is a proper funnel. Start with a much larger raw list, filter hard, score what's left, and only keep what genuinely qualifies.

---

## Step 1 — Build the Raw List First

I'd start by pulling somewhere between 4,000 and 5,000 company names. Not worrying about quality at this stage, just volume. Sources would include LinkedIn company searches, IndiaMART, TradeIndia, industry association directories, exporter databases, trade fair listings, and direct website discovery.

Sectors to focus on: Specialty Chemicals, Biotechnology, Medical Devices, Precision Engineering, Industrial Equipment, Advanced Manufacturing.

At this stage it's fine for the list to be messy. The filtering comes next.

---

## Step 2 — Remove the Obvious Non-Fits Fast

This is the rough but necessary pass. Companies that are clearly traders, distributors, import/export-only businesses, have no manufacturing footprint, appear inactive, or are in completely unrelated industries — out. This step alone probably cuts the list by half. It doesn't need to be precise, just fast.

Getting from 5,000 to maybe 2,000 here is a realistic target.

---

## Step 3 — Actually Research the Remaining Companies

For everything that survives the first cut, I'd go and actually look at each company properly. This means:

Finding the website and understanding what they really make (not just what the homepage says). Confirming that manufacturing actually happens in-house. Identifying who's running the company — specifically the Founder, CEO, or MD — and checking whether they have a technical background. Estimating revenue if any data is available anywhere. Looking for growth signals: new facilities, export mentions, certifications, hiring announcements.

AI tools can help speed up parts of this, especially for pulling basic company info. But leadership data specifically needs manual verification — automated tools get this wrong often enough that it's not worth trusting blindly.

---

## Step 4 — Score Every Company on the Federer Framework

Once data is collected, each company goes through the same six-criteria scoring system used in this round:

Is the manufacturing actually in-house? Is the company operating in India? Are the products specialized or commodity? Is the decision maker someone with real depth — technical background, founder, long-tenure executive? Is the sector growing? And are there visible company-level growth signals?

Companies that score well move forward. Companies that don't, get cut. The scoring should be applied consistently — the whole point of a framework is not making exceptions.

---

## Step 5 — Manual Review Before Locking the List

Borderline cases especially need a second look. A company that scores 45 out of 85 might still be a genuine fit that the scoring missed, or it might really just be a borderline company. That distinction matters.

I'd also check for duplicates (easy to get when pulling from multiple sources), missing information that should be filled in or flagged, and companies that got misclassified in earlier steps.

This step is slower but it's what separates a list that's actually useful from one that just looks complete.

---

## Timeline Across Four Weeks

Week 1 is building the raw database. Hit every source, collect names, get the initial data gathering underway.

Week 2 is the first filtering pass and deeper research on the companies that survive. Leadership details, manufacturing evidence, growth data.

Week 3 is running the Federer scoring across everything and working through borderline cases.

Week 4 is the final quality check, removing weak entries, filling gaps where possible, and formatting the final output for delivery.

---

## What to Expect at the End

Starting with 5,000 companies and filtering to 1,000 is roughly a 20% pass rate. That sounds low but it's actually about right given how specific the Federer criteria are. If the pass rate is higher than that, it probably means the filtering wasn't strict enough.

The 1,000 that make it through should be companies worth actually reaching out to — not just companies that technically exist and manufacture something.

---

## The Thing That Matters Most

Speed isn't the priority here. A list of 800 companies that genuinely fit is more valuable than a list of 1,200 where 400 of them don't really qualify. The goal is a database that can actually be used for outreach, not one that hits a number.

My approach would be structured research, AI assistance where it makes sense (data collection, initial screening), and manual judgment everywhere it doesn't. The combination is what makes the final output reliable.
