# Methodology

## What I Was Going For

The assignment was to find 25 companies that fit DeepThought's ICP. Simple enough on paper. But the tricky part was that "fits the ICP" is actually pretty specific — it's not just "manufactures something." The company needs to have in-house capabilities, visible growth, and ideally a founder or technical leader actually running things. So I had to be selective from the start, not just grab whatever company names I could find.

---

## Cities I Searched

I focused on manufacturing hubs rather than just metro cities. Places like Ahmedabad, Hyderabad, Bengaluru, Pune, Chennai, Coimbatore, Mumbai, Surat, Vadodara, and Madurai. The reason is pretty straightforward — if you want manufacturers, you go where manufacturing actually clusters. Coimbatore alone has a massive precision engineering ecosystem. Hyderabad has biotech and pharma. Ahmedabad has a mix of specialty chemicals and medical devices.

---

## Sources Used

Honestly, no single source gave everything I needed. I ended up using a combination:

- Company websites were always the first stop. If the website is just a landing page with no product details, that already tells you something.
- LinkedIn was probably the most useful for finding who's actually running the company, and whether that person has a real technical background or is just a hired executive.
- Annual reports (for the listed companies) gave revenue data, which most private companies don't publish anywhere.
- Industry reports filled in some gaps around sector trends.
- News articles and press releases helped spot growth signals — new plant announcements, export milestones, certifications, that kind of thing.

Private companies were harder. Some of them have almost no public presence beyond a basic website, which made it genuinely difficult to evaluate them.

---

## How the Research Actually Went

### Step 1 — Finding Companies

I started by identifying companies across manufacturing, biotech, medical devices, specialty chemicals, engineering, and pharma. Cast a wide net first, filter later. The early list was messy and included a lot of companies that ended up not qualifying.

### Step 2 — Screening Out the Non-Fits

Before going deep on any company, I did a quick check on four things: Do they actually manufacture in-house? Are they operating in India? Are their products specialized, or is it generic commodity stuff? And are they in a sector that's relevant?

If the answer to any of these was clearly no, I moved on. No point researching a trading company or a pure distributor.

### Step 3 — Finding the Decision Maker

For the companies that passed screening, I looked for the actual decision maker — usually a Founder, CEO, or MD. The title alone isn't enough though. A founder who built the company from scratch and understands the product is very different from a professional manager brought in to run operations. I tried to note that distinction wherever I could.

### Step 4 — Scoring on the Federer Framework

Each company was evaluated on six criteria: manufacturing capability, India presence, product differentiation, decision-maker quality, industry tailwinds, and growth signals. This is the same framework used across the assignment.

### Step 5 — Final Classification

After scoring, I assigned a Federer Score, a Score Band (A, B, or C), and a Verdict. I also wrote a Personalization Hook for each company — basically what you'd lead with if you were actually reaching out to that company.

---

## Things That Made This Hard

Revenue data was the biggest issue. Most of these companies are private, so you're making educated estimates based on whatever fragments are publicly available. Some of it is just not out there.

Leadership information also needed cross-referencing sometimes. LinkedIn profiles aren't always up to date, and a few companies had unclear org structures where it wasn't obvious who the real decision maker is.

And some companies just didn't have enough public information to evaluate properly. In those cases I noted the gap rather than guessing.

---

## What the Data Actually Showed

The pattern that emerged pretty clearly — the companies with the highest scores weren't necessarily the largest. What separated strong fits from borderline ones was usually whether the founder or a technical person was still actively running the business, whether the products were genuinely differentiated rather than commodity, and whether there were actual signs of growth happening (not just claims on the website).

Specialty chemicals, biotech, and medical devices consistently scored higher. Commodity manufacturers and traders almost never qualified. That tracks with what the framework is designed to filter for.

---

## Where It Ended Up

25 companies made the final list. I'm reasonably confident about the ones in the A band. The B and C band companies are included because they're legitimate manufacturers with real operations, but they had weaker differentiation or slower growth — not because they're irrelevant, just a step below the strongest fits.
